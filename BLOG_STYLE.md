# BLOG_STYLE.md

## 1. Project Context

This repository is used to write personal technology blogs.

The author is a software engineer and solution architect with around 8 years of experience building enterprise software, SaaS platforms and internal business systems.

Main experience areas:

* Software Architecture
* Multi-tenant SaaS
* Workflow & Approval Engine
* Inspection & Checklist System
* .NET / ASP.NET Core
* SQL Server / MongoDB
* Docker / K3s / Rancher
* DevOps & Home Lab
* AI for Developers
* Startup and B2B Software

The purpose of writing blogs is NOT:

* Showing off technical skills
* Chasing SEO traffic
* Writing tutorials copied from documentation
* Explaining frameworks mechanically

The purpose is:

* Organize knowledge and experiences
* Reflect on mistakes and lessons learned
* Share architectural thinking
* Build a trustworthy personal brand
* Help readers think more deeply about systems and technology

---

## 2. Author Identity

The author is NOT:

* A framework evangelist
* A technology preacher
* A motivational speaker
* A "hot take" social media influencer

The author IS:

* A builder
* A systems thinker
* A practical architect
* A problem solver
* Someone who values trade-offs and real-world constraints

Technology is never the main character.

The real main character is:

* Problems
* Systems
* Organizations
* Human behavior
* Trade-offs

---

## 3. Writing Style

Writing style:

### Calm

Avoid:

* Aggressive opinions
* Clickbait
* Excessive hype
* Emotional exaggeration

Prefer:

* Objective reasoning
* Real experiences
* Nuanced opinions
* Practical lessons

---

### Analytical

Always ask:

* What problem are we solving?
* Why is this difficult?
* What assumptions are hidden?
* What trade-offs exist?
* What happens at scale?
* What are the alternatives?

The goal is not to provide answers immediately.

The goal is to help readers think.

---

### Storytelling

Every article should feel like:

"A system architect sharing how he thinks."

Prefer:

* Real stories
* Mistakes
* Wrong assumptions
* Unexpected problems
* Lessons learned

Avoid:

* Dry theory
* Documentation style writing
* Academic explanations without context

---

### Practical

Readers should feel:

"I can apply this tomorrow."

Avoid:

* Pure theory
* Overly abstract concepts
* Unnecessary buzzwords

Always connect ideas with:

* Real systems
* Business scenarios
* Operational challenges
* Actual engineering decisions

---

## 4. Blog Philosophy

The blog is NOT about technology.

The blog is about:

How technology helps humans solve real problems.

Always remember:

A database is not interesting.

Why someone chooses SQL over MongoDB is interesting.

---

Kubernetes is not interesting.

Understanding when NOT to use Kubernetes is interesting.

---

DDD is not interesting.

Understanding whether the business is complex enough to justify DDD is interesting.

---

The deeper question is always:

What problem exists behind the technology?

---

## 5. Preferred Article Structure

Prefer this structure:

### 1. Hook

Start with:

* A mistake
* A surprising observation
* A real problem
* A wrong assumption
* An unexpected lesson

Examples:

"I once believed Kubernetes was necessary for every serious system."

"I thought Workflow Engine was just a sequence of approvals."

"For years I optimized database queries before realizing the bottleneck was organizational."

---

### 2. The Problem

Explain:

* Context
* Constraints
* Why simple solutions fail

---

### 3. Exploration

Discuss:

* Options
* Trade-offs
* Architecture decisions
* Why certain ideas failed

Be honest.

Include uncertainty.

---

### 4. Lessons Learned

Extract:

* Principles
* Mental models
* Design patterns
* Architectural insights

---

### 5. Conclusion

Do NOT summarize mechanically.

End with:

* A thought-provoking question
* A design principle
* A reflection

Readers should continue thinking after finishing the article.

---

## 6. Writing Rules

Always:

* Write naturally.
* Prefer short paragraphs.
* Prefer simple words.
* Explain jargon when necessary.
* Show trade-offs.
* Admit uncertainty.
* Share mistakes.

Never:

* Pretend to know everything.
* Claim one technology is universally best.
* Use excessive marketing language.
* Force humor.
* Use clickbait titles.
* Write like documentation.
